## BLS JOLTS: Annual Average Monthly Quit Rates — Information Sector (Official)

BLS classifies tech companies primarily under "Information" (NAICS 51). Rates are monthly averages; multiply by 12 for approximate annual voluntary turnover.

| Year | Information Sector Monthly Quit Rate | Annualized (~) | US Total Monthly Quit Rate |
|------|-------------------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| 2020 | 1.3% | ~15.6% | 2.1% (~25.2%) |
| 2021 | 1.8% | ~21.6% | 2.7% (~32.4%) |
| 2022 | 1.7% | ~20.4% | 2.8% (~33.6%) — Great Resignation peak |
| 2023 | 1.4% | ~16.8% | 2.4% (~28.8%) |
| 2024 | 1.4% | ~16.8% | 2.1% (~25.2%) |

The Information sector consistently runs **below** the US total quit rate, and below Leisure & Hospitality (3.9% monthly in 2024) and Professional & Business Services (2.4% monthly in 2024). The tech sector's quit rate has stabilized at the same level as 2020, suggesting the Great Resignation is fully unwound in tech.

## Key Findings

US tech voluntary turnover (~18.9% annually per industry aggregates, or ~16.8% annualized from BLS JOLTS "Information" sector monthly rates) has stabilized following the Great Resignation peak of 2021–2022. The BLS monthly quit rate for Information has held steady at 1.4%/month since 2023 — matching pre-pandemic levels. Technology consistently runs above the US average (13.2%) due to high workforce mobility, intense competition, and a Gen Z/Millennial-heavy demographic that turns over 2–3x faster than Gen X and Boomers. The single highest-leverage retention intervention is management quality — 71% of voluntary turnover is manager-attributable, and great managers reduce departure likelihood by 40%. Return-to-office mandates create meaningful attrition exposure: fully remote workers retain at 94.2% vs. 81.6% for office-based employees. The $28,900-per-employee retention cost in tech (highest of all industries) underlines the financial stakes of getting this right.
