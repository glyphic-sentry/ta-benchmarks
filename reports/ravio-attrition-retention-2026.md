## Compensation & Retention Link

- Employees paid above market (>55th percentile) have the lowest share of departures
- Competitive pay impact on retention is highest for Support roles and early-career (P1/P2)
- For Management and senior professionals (P3+), career progression and equity matter more than base salary
- 82% of People & Reward Leaders cite lack of clarity on career progression as their top retention challenge (vs. 61% citing compensation)

## Key Findings

Attrition across European tech has continued to normalize, reaching 17.4% in 2025 — down from pandemic highs of 27%. The most counterintuitive finding is that early-stage companies now have the *lowest* attrition (14.5%), despite cutting pay rises and promotions, suggesting equity upside and mission alignment are strong retention levers when comp budgets are tight. Late-stage companies are moving in the opposite direction (+18% YoY), likely reflecting role restructuring, slower growth, and employees reassessing their options as the market stabilizes. Operations is the most stressed function across all stages, with attrition surging 35% as AI and automation reshape routine tasks. Engineering continues to show the strongest retention at 12%, with engineers prioritizing job security after years of tech layoffs. Sweden's 28% attrition spike despite a low absolute rate (13%) illustrates a key principle: when market salaries rise but existing employee pay stagnates, departures follow.
